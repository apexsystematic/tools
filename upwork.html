04 Pricing Guidance

Upwork pricing has two phases: before your first 3 reviews, and after. Treat them differently.

Phase Hourly Rate Fixed Price Range Goal
0 reviews $35–45/hr $300–600 Win first job at any reasonable rate
1–2 reviews $45–60/hr $500–900 Start building rate floor
3–5 reviews $65–85/hr $800–1,500 Hit target Upwork range
5+ reviews $85–120/hr $1,200–2,500 Price-match your direct clients
Set your profile hourly rate at $55/hr on day one. This is your public rate. You can bid lower on fixed-price projects without it looking like a contradiction. $55/hr is credible for a specialist, not so high it filters you out at zero reviews, and signals you're not a $15/hr commodity. Once you have 3 reviews, raise it to $75. Once you have 5, raise it to $95+.
Fixed vs. Hourly: Prefer fixed-price on Upwork. It's easier to scope, clients feel less exposed, and you can complete a $500 project in 4 hours once you have the templates — that's $125/hr effective rate. Only agree to hourly if the scope is genuinely unclear and the client is sophisticated enough to understand that.
05 Proposal Template

Most Upwork proposals fail because they're about the freelancer, not the client. The formula that works: mirror their problem → show you've done it → explain how you'd solve theirs → clear next step. Keep it under 200 words. Never attach a generic cover letter.

Proposal Template — Adapt Per Job
Hi [Name], [MIRROR THEIR PROBLEM — 1–2 sentences. Restate what they described, in your own words, so they know you read it.] Example: "You're manually processing every new client intake and it's costing you time you could be billing." I've built this exact type of system — [brief reference to relevant portfolio item, 1 sentence]. The result was [concrete outcome: time saved, steps eliminated, etc.]. For your setup, I'd [describe the specific approach for their situation in 2–3 sentences. Name the tools. Be specific.]. To scope this properly, I'd want a 20-minute call to confirm a few details before quoting. I won't ask for money until I know exactly what needs building and you're happy with the scope. Fixed price, you own everything at the end. No retainer. Worth a quick call? Jorge
What makes this work: The mirror paragraph is the most important part. If your first two sentences are about you and not them, the proposal is dead. Clients skim — they want to know you understood their problem before they read anything else. The "no retainer" close is your differentiator and it handles an unspoken objection most clients have about hiring an automation freelancer. The question at the end ("Worth a quick call?") is lower friction than "Let me know if you'd like to connect."
Never do this: Don't open with "Hi, I'm Jorge and I specialise in automation." Don't list your skills before you've addressed their problem. Don't write more than 220 words. Don't attach a portfolio PDF — link to your Loom instead if relevant.

08 The First Job Playbook

Your first Upwork job has one objective: a 5-star review. Not profit. Not the perfect client. A clean, positive review that unlocks the rest of the platform for you. Everything about the first job should be oriented around this.

①
Target a small, well-scoped project ($250–500) Something you can deliver in under 8 hours. A single Make.com scenario, a simple intake flow, a one-step automation. Don't take on a complex multi-system build for your first job — too many variables, too much risk of scope creep, too easy to get a 4-star instead of 5.
②
Over-deliver on communication, not scope Send a message within 1 hour of being hired. Give a clear delivery timeline. Send a mid-project update. Deliver before the deadline. Ask if they have questions before you close the contract. Most 5-star reviews are about communication, not technical quality.
③
Write a handoff note, even for a small job A short bullet-point summary of what was built, how to use it, and what to do if something breaks. This takes 10 minutes and reads as professional and thorough. It's also a soft sell for your full documentation service on larger projects.
④
Ask for the review explicitly When you close the contract, send: "It was a pleasure working with you. If you have a moment to leave a review on Upwork, it would mean a lot — here's the link: [link]. I'm available for any follow-up questions." Direct, not pushy.
⑤
Raise your rate immediately after Even one 5-star review changes your positioning. Update your hourly rate the same day. One review is the difference between "unproven" and "has delivered."
09 Discovery Call Script

This is a 20–30 minute call. Your job is to listen 70% of the time, ask precise questions, and leave with enough information to write a scoped proposal. Do not pitch during this call. Do not quote a price until you've heard the answers to all the questions below.

Opening (2 min)
Thanks for making time — I'll keep this tight. I've got about 20 minutes of questions that should give me everything I need to put together a proper scope and quote for you. Sound good? [Let them confirm] Before I dive in — just to set expectations: I'm not going to pitch you on anything today. I'll ask questions, and if it sounds like automation is the right solution, I'll send you a written scope within 24 hours. If it's not the right fit, I'll tell you that too. Fair?
Problem Discovery (8–10 min)
1. Walk me through what you're doing manually right now that you want automated. Don't filter it — just describe it as if I'm watching over your shoulder. [Listen. Don't interrupt. Let them finish.] 2. How often does this happen? Daily, weekly, per client? 3. How long does it take you each time? 4. What tools are you already using? [e.g. Gmail, Airtable, a CRM, a forms tool, Calendly, etc.] 5. Do you have anyone else doing this task, or is it just you? 6. What happens when it goes wrong or gets missed? What's the actual cost of that? [This last question is the most important. The answer tells you how much this problem is worth to them — which anchors your pricing.]
Technical Readiness (5 min)
7. Do you currently use any automation tools — Make.com, Zapier, anything like that? 8. Who manages your tech stack? Is that you, or do you have someone else? 9. Are you comfortable setting up accounts and API connections, or would you need me to walk you through that? [Note: If they're non-technical, build extra time into your scope for handoff and documentation. Factor this into pricing.] 10. Do you have logins and admin access to the tools we'd be connecting? [This matters — if they need IT approval to connect apps, your timeline doubles.]
Fit & Timeline (3 min)
11. What's driving the timing on this? Is there a deadline, or is this more of a "when it's right" thing? 12. Have you tried to solve this before — either yourself or with someone else? What happened? [If they've tried before and it failed, find out why. Scope creep, bad communication, wrong tools — it tells you what to avoid and what to address in your proposal.] 13. Budget-wise — do you have a number in mind, or are you waiting to hear what it would cost? [Don't dodge this. If they have a number, get it on the table now. If they say "depends on the quote," that's fine — move on.]
Close (2 min)
That's everything I need. Based on what you've described, [brief honest assessment — e.g. "this is a straightforward single-scenario build" / "this is more complex than it might look because of X" / "I think there's a simpler solution than full automation here"]. I'll put together a written scope — what gets built, what tools, what the handoff looks like, and a fixed price. You'll have it within 24 hours. Any questions before I let you go? [If they ask for a ballpark price right now:] Rough range based on what you've described: $[X–Y]. The scope doc will nail it down exactly. I don't like quoting precisely without writing it out properly — it protects both of us.
After the call: Write your notes immediately. The key data points you need for the scope doc: the specific trigger (what starts the automation), the tools involved, the expected volume (how many times per day/week it runs), technical readiness of the client, and any red flags (vague scope, unclear ownership of tools, history of failed attempts). If you can't answer all of these from your notes, you didn't ask enough questions — send a follow-up message before writing the scope.
10 Scope Document Template

Send this within 24 hours of the discovery call. It's a plain document — not a formal contract, not a pitch deck. Its job is to prove you understood the problem and remove ambiguity before work starts. Clear scope = fewer disputes = better reviews.

Scope Document — Full Template
AUTOMATION SCOPE — [CLIENT NAME] Prepared by Jorge R. · Apex Systematic Date: [DATE] --- PROJECT SUMMARY [2–3 sentences describing what you're building and why. Write it so a non-technical person can read it and confirm "yes, that's what I want."] Example: "We're building an automated client intake flow that triggers when a new prospect submits your intake form. The system will create a client record in Airtable, send a branded confirmation email, and fire a Calendly booking link — without any manual steps on your end." --- WHAT'S INCLUDED ✓ [Specific deliverable 1 — e.g. Make.com scenario: Tally → Airtable → Gmail → Calendly] ✓ [Specific deliverable 2 — e.g. Airtable base setup with client record fields] ✓ [Specific deliverable 3 — e.g. Gmail template for confirmation email] ✓ End-to-end testing with live data ✓ Written handoff documentation (how it works, how to maintain it) ✓ Loom walkthrough video of the completed system ✓ 30-day check-in call --- WHAT'S NOT INCLUDED ✗ [Anything that came up in conversation but is out of scope — be explicit] ✗ Ongoing maintenance or monitoring after handoff ✗ Changes to scope after build begins (treated as a new project or change order) ✗ Design or branding work (email templates use your existing brand assets) --- TOOLS & ACCOUNTS This build uses the following tools. You will own all accounts: · Make.com (you create the account — I'll send setup instructions) · [Tool 2] · [Tool 3] All API keys and credentials will live in your accounts. I do not retain access after handoff. --- TIMELINE Discovery → Scope agreed: Done Build start: [DATE — typically within 2 business days of agreement] First draft for review: [DATE — typically 3–5 business days after start] Revisions: Up to 2 rounds included Final delivery: [DATE] --- INVESTMENT Fixed price: $[AMOUNT] Payment terms: 50% upfront via Upwork to start, 50% on delivery. This price covers everything listed in the "What's Included" section. Any additions to scope will be quoted separately before work begins. --- NEXT STEP If this looks right, reply with "approved" and I'll send the Upwork contract. If you have questions or want to adjust anything, reply and we'll sort it before we start. --- Jorge R. Apex Systematic apexsystematic.com
On the 50/50 payment split: Always. Even on small jobs. It filters out time-wasters, protects you against non-payment, and is standard practice — no legitimate client will push back on it. If someone refuses to pay anything upfront, that's a red flag, not a negotiation.
"What's Not Included" is the most important section. Scope creep kills small projects. Every time you've had a conversation where a client mentioned something adjacent to the main ask — write it in the exclusions. "We discussed the possibility of adding a Slack notification but this is not included in the current scope" protects you when they come back and say "I thought that was part of it." It's not confrontational — it's professional.
11 Maintenance Plan — Project Close Offer

This is an optional paid add-on offered only at handoff — never during the sale, never as a condition of the build. It must not conflict with your "no retainer, you own it" positioning. Frame it as peace of mind, not dependency.

When to Offer It
Offer the maintenance plan during the 30-day check-in call, not at project close. By then the client has seen the system running, understands its value, and has a sense of whether they want ongoing support. Offering it at handoff — before they've experienced the system — feels like upselling. Offering it at the 30-day mark feels like a natural conversation. The check-in agenda: 1. Is everything running as expected? 2. Any edge cases we haven't handled? 3. Any new processes you want to look at automating? 4. [If relevant] "Do you want me to keep an eye on this going forward? I offer a light monthly maintenance plan — let me tell you what's included."
Maintenance Plan — Offer Script
Now that the system's been running for a month — the main thing that breaks automations isn't the logic, it's the tools underneath. Make.com updates, API changes, a third-party app changes its field names. It usually takes 15–30 minutes to fix when it happens, but you'd need to either do it yourself or come back to me each time on an ad-hoc basis. If you'd rather not think about it, I offer a simple monthly plan: I monitor your scenario, handle any breaks caused by tool changes, and you get priority turnaround if something goes wrong. It's $[X]/month, covers up to [Y] hours of maintenance work, and you can cancel any time with 30 days notice. It's not for everyone — some clients are technical enough to handle this themselves and prefer it that way. But if you'd rather focus on your work and not on whether your automation is still running, it might be worth it. Want me to send you the details?
Maintenance Plan — Pricing Structure
TIER 1 — Basic Monitoring: $79/month · Monthly scenario health check · Fix breaks caused by tool/API changes (up to 1 hour/month) · Email support, 48hr response · Best for: simple single-scenario automations, low volume TIER 2 — Active Support: $149/month · All of Tier 1 · Up to 3 hours maintenance/fixes per month · Priority response (same business day) · Quarterly optimisation review call (30 min) · Best for: multi-scenario builds, client-facing automations TIER 3 — Full Cover: $249/month · All of Tier 2 · Up to 6 hours maintenance/fixes per month · Minor enhancements included (e.g. adding a new field, adjusting logic) · Monthly usage report · Best for: business-critical automations, high-volume scenarios Additional hours beyond the included allowance: $65/hr All plans: cancel with 30 days notice. No lock-in.
Cost check before you sell this: Make.com costs are per operation, not per client. Before quoting a maintenance plan, check how many operations your build runs per month on the client's account. A scenario running 500 operations/month at Make.com's Core plan is essentially free overhead on your end. A high-volume scenario running 50,000 operations/month on a plan you're managing is a real cost. Know the numbers before you price. The tiers above assume low-to-medium volume scenarios where your actual cost exposure per client is under $15/month.
Positioning note: When you describe this plan, always use the phrase "cancel any time." It's the verbal proof that this isn't a retainer trap. Your "no retainer" brand promise applies to the build — this is a separate, optional service the client chooses to continue. The distinction is real and you should make it explicit if a client asks.
Never do this: Don't mention the maintenance plan during the initial sales conversation. Don't imply the automation will break without ongoing support. Don't bundle it into the project price as a "first month included." All of these undermine the trust that "you own it outright" creates — and that trust is your primary differentiator.
Bottom line Profile built for the niche. Proposal built for the problem. First job built for the review. Discovery call built for the scope. Scope doc built for trust. Maintenance plan offered after proof, not before. In that order. Don't skip steps.
