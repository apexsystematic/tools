Foundation
Positioning & Messaging
Core Value Proposition — One Sentence
FutureProof gives senior professionals their personal AI displacement risk score, a precise timeline, and a first-move plan — built around their actual work, not their job title.
The 3 Pain Points This Report Solves
01
Generic Risk = No Actionable Signal
Every AI risk tool outputs by job title. Two directors at the same company with the same title can have completely different exposure profiles. Generic answers breed paralysis, not decisions.
02
Calibration Error in Both Directions
High-performers routinely misread their own risk — underestimating it because they use AI tools, or overestimating it because they're in a disrupted sector. Both errors are costly. This report corrects both.
03
Plans That Don't Fit Real Lives
Career advice assumes unlimited time. Senior professionals have 1-2 hours a week, not 10. The FutureProof plan is built around actual availability — because a plan that doesn't fit your life isn't a plan.
Primary Emotional Trigger — Lead With This
Fear of invisible risk — not fear of AI itself. Your buyer isn't afraid of AI in the abstract. They're afraid of being wrong. Of making a major career decision without the right data. Of watching the layer below them get eliminated and not knowing if they're next. The trigger is epistemic anxiety: "I don't actually know my number, and that uncertainty is costing me." Lead with clarity as the antidote, not alarm as the hook.
Tone of Voice — Rules of the Voice
Do Precise and data-grounded. Cite specific numbers, percentages, timeframes. The authority comes from specificity.
Don't Apocalyptic AI-doom framing. "AI will destroy your career" repels your audience. They've heard it. It's noise.
Do Calm confidence — the tone of someone who has seen the data and knows what it says. Steady, not breathless.
Don't Corporate consultancy-speak. No "leverage synergies," no "future-ready workforce." Human language only.
Do Personal and first-person. You built this for your wife. That story is the emotional core of everything.
Don't Hype and urgency tactics. No countdown timers, no "limited spots," no manufactured scarcity. It cheapens the product.
Do Name the insight precisely. "Using AI tools is not the same as being safe from them" is better than "many people misunderstand AI risk."
Don't Over-explain the methodology. The product is the output — a number, a timeline, a first move. Trust the reader to trust the result.

Who You're Talking To
Target Audience
Primary Buyer Persona
The Senior Professional in a Disrupted Sector
Director / VP / Senior Manager · 35–52 · $120K–$250K total comp
Role Profile
  • 10+ years in their domain. Credentialed. Respected internally.
  • Works in a sector with visible AI activity: pharma, consulting, finance, marketing, legal, healthcare administration.
  • Manages a team or a complex portfolio of work — not execution-only.
  • Has invested heavily in their career: postgrad degrees, certifications, professional networks.
Core Fears
  • Being wrong about their exposure — in either direction.
  • Making a panic move that destroys seniority and equity they've built.
  • Watching colleagues act and not knowing if they should too.
  • Realising too late that the window to reposition closed.
Motivations
  • Wants clarity, not content. They've read 40 articles. None gave them an answer.
  • Willing to pay for signal vs. noise — they spend $995 on a dinner, a flight, a course without thinking.
  • Wants a plan they can actually execute — not a 60-page PDF.
  • Respects specificity. Vague advice insults their intelligence.
Secondary Persona
  • The concerned partner — spouse or partner of a senior professional who wants an objective answer to bring to their household conversation.
  • Highly motivated buyer. Emotional purchase driver. Often the one who shares the post that converts.
Where They Spend Time Online
1st LinkedIn — professional identity, career news, industry updates. This is where they go when something at work makes them anxious.
2nd Newsletters — Morning Brew, The Hustle, industry-specific digests. Passive consumption but high attention.
Low Twitter / X — declining professional use. Lots of noise, low signal for senior B2C buyers.
Skip TikTok / Instagram — wrong context entirely. These platforms signal amateur, not premium advisor.
Content They Already Consume
They read: McKinsey / WEF AI reports (but can't personalise them), sector-specific trade publications, thoughtful LinkedIn essays from practitioners (not influencers), HBR articles on leadership and career strategy. They listen to podcasts during commutes — mostly business/strategy-oriented. The content gap they feel is: all of this gives them macro data, none of it gives them their own number. That gap is your product.

Content Strategy
Platform Focus — Where to Put Your Hours
Primary
LinkedIn — 90% of your content effort
Your buyer is a senior professional who uses LinkedIn as their professional nervous system. They check it when something at work makes them anxious — which is exactly the moment you want to appear. LinkedIn's algorithm currently rewards personal, narrative-driven text posts more than any other format, and your origin story is purpose-built for this. You already have a two-week content calendar live. The edge here is consistency and insight depth, not production value.
Secondary
Email — build from day one, even with zero subscribers
Every person who takes the free score assessment is a potential subscriber. A simple fortnightly email (Substack or Brevo — you already have Brevo) keeps FutureProof top of mind between LinkedIn sessions. Cost: 1–2 hours every two weeks. This becomes your most valuable owned asset over 6–12 months. Start it even with 5 subscribers.
4-Week Content Themes
Week 01
Origin & Proof of Concept
Establish who you are and why this exists. The wife story. The first case study. A clean CTA. Goal: earn the right to be taken seriously. 3 posts.
Week 02
Depth & Differentiation
Show the nuance — overestimating risk is as dangerous as underestimating it. The real-constraints angle. The price reveal. Goal: create "this is different" recognition. 3 posts.
Week 03
Education & Industry Signals
Teach something. Share a sector-specific insight. Comment on a relevant news event through the FutureProof lens. Goal: attract followers who aren't yet buyers. 3 posts.
Week 04
Social Proof & Invitation
First client testimonial (if available). A "what I've learned from N assessments" synthesis post. A direct invitation to book. Goal: convert warm followers. 3 posts.
Cadence: 3 posts per week — Monday, Wednesday, Friday. Plain text for narrative/insight posts. Canva graphic for data posts. Never post more than once per day. Quality over volume always wins at this price point.
5 Specific Post Ideas — Hooks Written Out
1
Week 3 · Education · Plain Text
"The most dangerous sentence in a McKinsey AI report is: 'roles involving X are at high risk of automation.' Here's what it doesn't tell you — and why that gap might be costing you."
Unpacks the difference between role-level risk and task-level exposure. Positions FutureProof as solving the exact gap McKinsey creates. Attracts senior readers who've read those reports and felt the frustration.
2
Week 3 · Industry Signal · Plain Text
"I've now assessed professionals across [N] sectors. One pattern keeps showing up that surprised me: the people most certain about their AI risk are almost always wrong — but not randomly wrong."
Shares a counterintuitive finding from the assessment data. Builds epistemic authority. Draws in readers who are curious about the pattern before revealing it. The hook creates a knowledge gap the post fills.
3
Week 3 · Hot Take · Plain Text
"Taking an AI course to protect your job is often the wrong move. Here's when it helps, and when it's just expensive noise."
Challenges the dominant career advice narrative (take courses, upskill constantly). Contrarian but data-backed. Will trigger engagement from people who have taken courses and wonder if it mattered. Positions FutureProof as the calibration tool.
4
Week 4 · Social Proof · Plain Text
"A client told me after receiving her report: 'I've spent 18 months worrying about the wrong thing.' Her score was 22/100. Here's what changed for her in the following week."
Testimonial-adjacent — leads with the client's words, then narrates the behavioural shift. Avoids the stiffness of a direct testimonial quote. The "22/100" number creates recognition for anyone who's seen prior posts (continuity reward for followers).
5
Week 4 · Direct Invite · Plain Text
"I have capacity for [N] new full assessments this month. If you've been thinking about it — here's exactly what you get, what it costs, and what happens after you order."
A transparent, non-pushy direct offer. The candour about capacity (real or approximate) signals premium scarcity without manufactured urgency. "What happens after you order" reduces purchase anxiety by making the process visible. Strong conversion post for warm followers.

Buyer Psychology
Objection Handling
💬
"$995 is a lot to spend on a report I've never heard of, from someone I just found on LinkedIn."
The Real Objection
This is a trust deficit, not a price objection. The buyer doesn't doubt the value of the information — they doubt your ability to deliver it accurately. The fix is credibility density in your content, not price reduction. They need to see enough case studies, enough specific numbers, and enough demonstrated insight that paying $995 feels safer than not knowing.
Preemptive Content Answer
Lead with outcomes, not process. Every case study post shows a real score, a real insight gap, a real strategy change. Before anyone sees the price, they've already seen the product working. By week 4, the price post is the sixth piece of content — not the first thing they encounter. Credibility is front-loaded. Price is back-loaded.
💬
"I can get AI career risk information for free. Why would I pay nearly $1,000 for a report?"
The Real Objection
This buyer has confused data availability with insight quality. They've read the McKinsey reports and the LinkedIn articles. None of them gave them their number. The objection answers itself — they've already tried the free options and are still uncertain. The job of your content is to make that gap visible without condescending.
Preemptive Content Answer
The McKinsey insight gap post (Post Idea #1) is designed exactly for this objection. Use language like: "The WEF report tells you your sector is at risk. It cannot tell you if you are." Name the gap explicitly, then name FutureProof as what fills it. Never disparage free tools — just clarify what they don't do.
💬
"How do I know the score is accurate? This feels like it could be just another AI output."
The Real Objection
This is the most legitimate objection. It deserves a real answer, not a deflection. The buyer is right to ask it. The answer lies in the methodology — task-level analysis, not title-level — and in the delivery format: a detailed, reasoned report, not a score generator. The fact that you personally deliver and review every report is a competitive advantage in a world of automated tools.
Preemptive Content Answer
Write one post specifically about the methodology. Not in technical detail, but in outcome terms: "We don't ask what your job title is. We ask how you actually spend your working week, task by task. Then we model each task's automation exposure — not based on sector trends, but on what's already available commercially today." This makes the methodology legible without being a spec sheet.

Conversion Strategy
The Conversion Funnel — How Followers Become Buyers
Stage 1 · Awareness
Post surfaces in their feed
A specific, insightful post — a case study, a counterintuitive finding, the origin story — stops the scroll. They don't buy here. They follow, save, or comment. Your only job at this stage is to be worth following.
Stage 2 · Curiosity
They take the free score
The free score is your best conversion tool. It gives the buyer a taste of personalisation — their number, not a generic assessment — at zero cost and zero commitment. The score creates a desire for explanation: "What does 51/100 actually mean for me?" That's the purchase motive.
Stage 3 · Warm Engagement
DM or comment signals intent
When someone comments "this is exactly where I am" or DMs you after a post, they've self-identified. Respond personally. Don't pitch. Ask a question about their situation. The conversation earns the right to mention the report — never the other way around.
Stage 4 · Decision
Week 4 content closes the loop
By week 4, followers have seen 9–11 posts. The direct offer (Post Idea #5) lands on a warm audience. The capacity framing ("I have room for N assessments this month") creates legitimate scarcity without manufactured urgency. The transparent process description removes final purchase anxiety.
Stage 5 · Retention & Referral
Client becomes a case study and referrer
Your post-delivery workflow (feedback questionnaire, delivery email) is already built. Add one question: "Is there a colleague or peer you think would benefit from knowing their number?" A warm referral from a satisfied client converts at 3–5x the rate of cold organic traffic.
CTA Language — What Works at a Premium Price Point
Primary CTA — Free score entry point
"Know your number before you make any career decisions. Free, two minutes, no email required to see your result. Link in comments."
Low friction. No commitment. Positions the score as due diligence, not a sales funnel. "Before you make any career decisions" implies they already have decisions to make — which is true.
Secondary CTA — Warm audience / week 4 offer
"If your score is above 50 and you want the full picture — your timeline, your credential moat assessment, and a first-move plan built around your actual life — that's the FutureProof Executive Audit. $995. Link in comments."
Names the price directly. Names the product. "Built around your actual life" echoes the real-constraints angle that resonates with the persona. Only use this after two weeks of credibility content — never as a cold open.
DM CTA — One-to-one conversion
"Happy to share more about what the full report covers — not a pitch, just want to make sure it's actually the right fit for your situation before you spend anything."
The "right fit" framing is powerful at premium prices. It signals that you're selective, not desperate. It reduces buyer pressure. It builds trust precisely because it doesn't push.
Where to Place the Offer in Organic Content
Rule 1: Put the URL in the first comment, never in the post body. LinkedIn suppresses reach on posts with external links.

Rule 2: Every post that mentions FutureProof should include the free score CTA. Not every post needs to pitch the $995 report.

Rule 3: The ratio is 80/20. 80% of posts are purely educational or storytelling — no CTA. 20% include a soft or hard offer. Buyers need to encounter your thinking many times before they encounter your price.

Rule 4: When you do offer the paid product, do it plainly. No hype, no FOMO, no countdown. "Here's what it costs, here's what you get, here's the link." Seniors find pressure tactics condescending.

Realistic Conversion Benchmarks — Cold Organic Audience
Month 1 target: 1 paid report. If your first 3 weeks generate 100+ free score takers, 1–2% will convert to conversations. Of those conversations, 20–30% will close if handled well. At 100 score takers, that's 1–2 conversations, and 1 likely sale. This is a realistic, not pessimistic, benchmark for cold organic — do not measure month 1 against month 6 expectations.

Month 2–3 target: 2–4 paid reports per month. By month 2, you have a content library that continues generating impressions, a small but warm follower base, and your first testimonial. Conversion rate climbs as social proof compounds.

The non-obvious benchmark: Track the ratio of saves-to-impressions on your posts, not likes-to-impressions. Save rate above 0.5% indicates your content is attracting buyers, not just readers. Posts with high save rates should be doubled down on.
When to Pivot vs. Stay the Course
Stay the Course — These are Normal
  • No sales in week 1–2. Too early. Trust hasn't been earned yet.
  • Low follower growth in week 1. LinkedIn growth is non-linear — it spikes.
  • Uneven post performance. Some posts will flop. Post 9 can outperform posts 1–8 combined.
  • Slow score-taker growth. Attribution from LinkedIn to your site takes 3–4 weeks to establish.
  • A case study post underperforms. Try a different sector next time. The format is right; the vertical needs testing.
Pivot Signal — Act on These
  • Zero DMs or comments after 4 full weeks. Your content isn't creating recognition or resonance. Rewrite the hooks.
  • Profile views not growing after week 3. Your content is reaching people but not making them curious about you. Revisit the About section and headline.
  • Score takers with zero report interest after 60+ takers. The gap between free tool and paid product is too wide. Add a follow-up email after score delivery that bridges the gap.
  • Engagement from wrong audience (students, juniors, recruiters). Your targeting language needs adjustment — add "senior," "director-level," "decade of experience" to your framing.
The 90-day rule: Do not evaluate this strategy before 90 days of consistent, weekly execution. LinkedIn organic growth is a compounding curve — it looks flat for 6–8 weeks, then accelerates. Every creator who abandons a LinkedIn strategy does so in the flat period, weeks before the inflection point. Commit to the full 90 days, track the leading indicators (saves, DMs, score takers), and make adjustments to format and hook — not to the channel or the price.
